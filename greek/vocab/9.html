δημοκρατία, -ας, -η

Etymology

The word δημοκρατία is derived from two Ancient Greek words:

  • δῆμος (dêmos): meaning “common people” or “assembly of the people.”
  • -κρατία (-kratía): meaning “power” or “rule.”

Thus, δημοκρατία literally translates to “rule by the people” or "people power"12.

Historical Context

The concept of δημοκρατία first emerged in ancient Athens during the 5th century BCE. It was a revolutionary idea at the time, proposing that the power to govern should lie in the hands of the people rather than a monarch or a small elite group3. This form of government allowed citizens to participate directly in decision-making processes, a stark contrast to the autocratic systems prevalent in other parts of the world.

Interesting Facts

  1. First Use: The term δημοκρατία was first used to describe the political system of Athens around 507 BCE, under the leadership of Cleisthenes, who is often referred to as "the father of Athenian democracy"3.
  2. Influence on Modern Governments: The principles of Athenian democracy have significantly influenced modern democratic systems. Concepts such as equal participation, civic duty, and the rule of law can trace their roots back to ancient Greece2.
  3. Philosophical Debates: Ancient philosophers like Plato and Aristotle had complex views on democracy. While they acknowledged its potential for fairness and equality, they also warned of its susceptibility to demagoguery and mob rule3.
  4. Evolution of the Term: Over centuries, the term δημοκρατία has evolved and been adopted by many languages, reflecting the spread and adaptation of democratic ideals worldwide. For instance, it became “democratia” in Latin, “démocratie” in French, and “democracy” in English45.

Pronunciation and Usage

In Ancient Greek, δημοκρατία was pronounced as /dɛː.mo.kra.tí.aː/ in 5th century BCE Attic Greek. Over time, the pronunciation evolved, and in Modern Greek, it is pronounced as /ði.mo.kraˈti.a/1.

δέ = (conjunction) and, but

Historical Context and Usage

In Ancient Greek, δέ is a postpositive particle, meaning it never appears as the first word in a sentence. Instead, it typically occupies the second position, though it can sometimes be found in the third or fourth position1. It is often used in conjunction with μέν (mén) to create a contrast or to add emphasis, similar to the English “on the one hand… on the other hand…” construction1.

Interesting Facts

  1. Versatility: δέ can be translated into English in various ways depending on the context, including “but,” “and,” “now,” or "on the other hand"3. This versatility makes it a crucial element in Greek syntax and discourse.
  2. Descendants: The particle δέ has influenced several languages. For example, it appears in Classical Syriac as ܕܝܢ and in Coptic as ⲇⲉ (de)1.
  3. Pronunciation Evolution: The pronunciation of δέ has evolved over time. In 5th century BCE Attic Greek, it was pronounced /dé/. By the 1st century CE in Egyptian Greek, it was /de/, and in later periods, it shifted to /ðe/1.

Pronunciation Over Time

  • 4th century CE Koine: /ðe/
  • 10th century CE Byzantine: /ðe/

Usage Notes

  • Postpositive Nature: δέ is never the first word in a sentence. It usually follows the first word or phrase, adding nuance or contrast to the statement1.

Etymology

The word ὑπό comes from the Proto-Indo-European root *upó, meaning “under” or "below"12. This root is also the source of several cognates in other languages, such as:

  • Latin: sub
  • Sanskrit: उप (upa)
  • Old English: under (modern English “under”)12

Historical Context and Usage

In Ancient Greek, ὑπό is used as a preposition governing the genitive, dative, and accusative cases. It has various meanings depending on the case it governs12:

  • With Genitive: Indicates origin, cause, or agent. For example, “ὑπὸ τοῦ διδασκάλου” (by the teacher).
  • With Dative: Indicates position or means. For example, “ὑπὸ τῷ δένδρῳ” (under the tree).
  • With Accusative: Indicates motion towards or extent. For example, “ὑπὸ τὴν γῆν” (under the earth).

Interesting Facts

  2. Derived Terms: Many Greek words are derived from ὑπό, such as ὕπᾰτος (húpatos, “highest”) and ὕπτιος (húptios, “lying on one’s back”)1.
  3. Modern Descendants: The prefix hypo- in English, used in words like “hypodermic” (under the skin) and “hypothermia” (below normal temperature), is derived from ὑπό1.
  4. Pronunciation Evolution: The pronunciation of ὑπό has evolved over time. In 5th century BCE Attic Greek, it was pronounced /hy.pó/. By the 1st century CE in Egyptian Greek, it was /hyˈpo/, and in later periods, it shifted to /iˈpo/1.
